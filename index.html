Introduction

In this project, we will look at the Sum and Product Puzzle, a logic puzzle introduced by Hans Freudenthal in 1969. This riddle is also known as the Impossible Puzzle, as it seems to require more information to be solvable. At first glance, it may appear that the statements from the agents are not informative. By closer examination using epistemic logic, it turns out solving this riddle is in fact possible, although not easily.


The (translated) original formulation (Freudenthal, 1969) of this riddle consists of the following:

Agent A tells agent S and P the following statement: "I have chosen two whole integers x, y such that 1 < x < y and x+y ≤ 100. In a moment, I will inform S only of s = x + y, P of p = xy. These announcements remain private. However, you are required to calculate the pair (x, y)."

Agent A acts as said. The following conversation happens:


    1. P says ”I do not know it.”
    2. S says ”I knew you didn’t.”
    3. P says ”I now know it.”
    4. S says ”I now also know it.”

Determine the pair (x, y).


What Freudenthal (1969) did not explicitly mention in his original formulation was the assumption that S and P are two mathematicians and consequently, perfect logicians who always tell the truth. In our project, we will adopt this assumption. Therefore the announcements in the conversation are common knowledge. It now turns out that the statements by P and S are informative. As a result of announcement 1, it is for instance commonly known that the pair (x, y) = (3,5) is impossible. The values in the pair are prime numbers and if it was the case that (x, y) = (3,5) then P would have said "I do know it". This way, it is possible to eliminate different pairs after each public announcement such that they both know the correct answer.


We have developed a riddle solver which is able to provide a correct solution to this puzzle and some variations to this puzzle. This includes the Sum and Product Puzzle for different values, but also broader generalisations which you will see under 'variations'. The puzzle involves advanced topics of logic, as the (higher-order) knowledge changes after each public announcement. For instance, the knowledge of S changes after the first statement from P. Analyses of this kind can be made by our solver. We will make use of the public announcement logic and semantics as taught in the LAMAS-course.
